The problem
2 in 5of Africa's giraffe, gone in a single generation, a silent extinction almost no one was counting.

We are losing wildlife faster than we can count it.

  • Giraffe: ~140,000 left, down roughly 40% in 30 years, with 90% of habitat lost. Some subspecies are now critically endangered.
  • Conservation still counts individual animals largely by hand: slow, costly and fragmented. You cannot protect what you cannot count.
  • The world is finally demanding credible biodiversity data: TNFD, 1,200+ nature-positive commitments, and almost no one can supply it at the level of the individual animal.
The populations

Four species, heading in different directions.

About 140,000 giraffe remain, up from under 100,000 in 2016, first because the data improved, and now because conservation is genuinely working: three of the four species are increasing. The longer story is the range itself, nearly 90% of it already gone.

Where protection is funded, tracked and acted on, giraffe recover, that is the lesson of the last decade. The ones still slipping away are the ones we cannot yet see clearly. The difference is data, and that is exactly what Patterns produces.

Why now

A coat is a fingerprint. A phone is a sensor. Carry the wild home with you.

  • Every giraffe, zebra, whale and leopard carries a unique, lifelong marking pattern.
  • Modern computer vision identifies an individual from one photo, in under a second.
  • Millions of wildlife photos are taken every year. Nobody is structuring them.
  • Citizen science already rewrote the map. eBird, iNaturalist and Happywhale turned everyday photos into the largest wildlife datasets on Earth. Giraffe numbers were revised upward only because the data improved.
The engine

One photo in.
The exact individual out.

Every photo runs a multi-stage computer-vision pipeline: instance segmentation isolates the animal, the coat is lifted into hundreds of scale-invariant keypoints and encoded as high-dimensional descriptors, matched against tens of thousands of references with approximate-nearest-neighbour vector search, then confirmed by geometric, ratio-tested verification and a calibrated confidence score.

Detection and matching run as cloud inference workers against a warm, in-memory vector index; the catalogue is the source of truth and sharpens with every verified sighting. The methodology builds on the field's gold-standard work, Microsoft's AI for Earth wildlife re-identification and the Wildbook research network, advanced into a real-time engine and tuned on live African field photography, built to a standard that makes rebuilding it pointless.

The hard part is done

      
The hard part is built. The next hard part is reach.

      
What looks like a photo app is a multi-stage computer-vision pipeline, a continuously-learning catalogue, a data-integrity backbone and an engagement system, each a serious project in its own right. That is the hard part, and it is already built and live.

      

        
    
          
  • The dataset compounds. Every sighting curates a living record of who, where and when. It grows richer and more valuable with every photo taken.
    • 
          
  • Field-grade accuracy, earned. Identifying an individual from a real safari photo, half-behind a tree, is genuinely hard. Ours is tuned and calibrated against a real catalogue.
    • 
        

        
    
          
  • Conservation-grade integrity. Every record is human-verifiable and auditable, not a fun guess. That is what parks and researchers can actually trust.
    • 
          
  • The engagement engine. Citizen science fails on participation, not technology. The collectible, the reveal, the joy: that is what gets people contributing.
    • 
        

      

      
Building the engine took years. The next mountain is reach: getting Patterns into the hands of enough citizen scientists to feed it. That is exactly where the right partners come in.

    

  


  
  

    

      
The reframe

      
Not a safari app. A wildlife-data platform with a consumer front-end.

      

      

        
The app is the cheapest data-collection sensor network and the most shareable conservation story in the category. The value is the structured, individually-identified sightings it produces, the data parks and researchers have never had.

        
Every serious wildlife-ID effort, from Happywhale to Wildbook, exists to serve conservation, not to sell downloads. Patterns is the same: the app is the means, the species is the point.

Where the data goes

      
The data belongs to the giraffe. So it is free to those who protect them.

      
Every verified sighting, location and movement track is handed to giraffe conservation, parks and reserves, free of charge. No paywall on the science. That is the whole point.

      

        

          
A self-sustaining loop
